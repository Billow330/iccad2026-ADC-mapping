\title{Measured Layer Sensitivity Guides Mixed-Precision ADC Allocation for CIM-Based LLM Inference}

\author{Anonymous Author(s)}

\begin{abstract}
Compute-in-Memory (CIM) architectures accelerate large language model (LLM) inference by performing transformer-layer matrix-vector multiplications directly in resistive memory arrays, but their analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) dominate chip area.
Mixed-precision ADC allocation can reduce this cost, yet conventional proxy signals---saturation rate and Hessian trace---do not reliably predict which transformer layers tolerate lower ADC precision in the CIM setting.
We directly profile per-layer-type sensitivity under ADC bit reduction and uncover a \emph{saturation--sensitivity inversion}: the highest-saturation attention layers ($W_{qkv}$) are the \emph{least} accuracy-sensitive, while low-saturation FFN layers ($W_{fc2}$) are the most---the opposite of what conventional proxies suggest.
Based on this finding, we propose a low-cost profiling-guided allocation flow that extracts the sensitivity signal in $G{+}1$ evaluation runs and feeds it to an area-aware integer linear program (ILP).
On OPT-125M, the flow outperforms saturation-guided, Hessian-guided, and random baselines under the same ILP solver, achieving 20\% ADC-area savings at under 1\% relative perplexity degradation.
On OPT-1.3B, it outperforms saturation-guided allocation at both tested budgets.
Transfer sanity checks confirm that the sensitivity ordering generalizes to higher-bit deployment regimes with negligible accuracy cost.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_methodology}
  \caption{Proposed allocation flow. (1)~CIM simulation evaluates LLM layers with configurable per-layer ADC precision. (2)~Direct group-level profiling measures $\Delta$PPL per layer group by lowering one group at a time. (3)~An area-aware ILP assigns ADC bits under an ADC-area budget, reducing low-sensitivity $W_{qkv}$ layers to 6-bit while keeping sensitive FFN/output groups at 7-bit. (4)~NeuroSIM validates the resulting accuracy, ADC-area, energy, and latency trade-offs.}
  \label{fig:overview}
\end{figure}

CIM architectures~\cite{chi2016prime,shafiee2016isaac} accelerate LLM inference by performing matrix-vector multiplications directly in resistive memory arrays.
The ADCs that digitize the accumulated column currents grow exponentially in area with bit resolution: NeuroSIM~\cite{chen2018neurosim} reports that ADCs occupy 24.4\% of total chip area at 7-bit and 63.9\% at 10-bit for OPT-125M~\cite{zhang2022opt}.
Mixed-precision ADC allocation---assigning fewer bits to tolerant layers---can substantially reduce this cost.
The central challenge is identifying which layers can tolerate reduced precision.

Existing digital mixed-precision methods~\cite{dong2019hawq,wang2019haq} rely on Hessian traces or saturation rates to guide bit allocation.
In the CIM ADC regimes we study, however, these proxies fail to predict measured sensitivity: saturation rate is anti-correlated and Hessian trace is uncorrelated with directly measured accuracy impact (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
A conventional saturation-guided strategy would therefore protect the wrong layers.

We address this gap by directly measuring per-layer-type sensitivity under ADC bit reduction, revealing a \emph{saturation--sensitivity inversion}: the highest-saturation layers ($W_{qkv}$) are the least sensitive, while the lowest-saturation layers ($W_{fc2}$) are the most.
This measured signal is low-cost ($G{+}1$ evaluation runs) and transferable---low-bit profiling produces the same group-level ILP allocations when deployed at higher-bit regimes in all evaluated settings.
Using this signal, the proposed allocation flow outperforms all tested proxy baselines under the same area budget (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}).

\textit{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We show that saturation and Hessian proxies fail to predict ADC sensitivity in the CIM setting, and that direct profiling reveals an opposite ranking---validated on OPT-125M/1.3B and confirmed directionally on Pythia-410M and Qwen2-7B.

  \item We propose a group-level profiling protocol (Algorithm~\ref{alg:sensitivity}) that extracts the allocation signal in $G{+}1$ evaluation runs. The resulting ordering is stable across four operating points and transfers to higher-bit deployment with zero group-level regret.

  \item Under the same ILP solver, measured sensitivity consistently yields better allocations than all tested proxy signals on OPT-125M (three baselines) and OPT-1.3B (saturation baseline), including at practical higher-bit regimes.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{CIM Array Architecture and ADC Bottleneck}

Fig.~\ref{fig:cim_architecture}a groups decoder-only LLM linear projections into the sensitivity groups used in this work, while Fig.~\ref{fig:cim_architecture}b shows their mapping to CIM subarrays.
In a CIM crossbar, weights $\mathbf{W}$ are programmed as conductance states in resistive memory cells.
The input vector $\mathbf{x}$ is applied row-wise through DACs, and column currents accumulate according to Kirchhoff's current law before being digitized by ADCs.
As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:cim_architecture}c, for an $M$-column subarray with a $b$-bit MLSA-type ADC~\cite{chen2018neurosim}, the ADC area scales as
\begin{equation}
  A_\text{ADC} \propto M \cdot 2^b
  \label{eq:adc_area}
\end{equation}
because the multi-level sense amplifier requires $2^b$ comparator levels.
Across all 73 linear layers of OPT-125M (4 unique weight-matrix shapes), this exponential scaling produces the area breakdown shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:ppa_sweep}, where the ADC share grows from 24\% at 7-bit to 64\% at 10-bit.

\subsection{Activation Outliers and ADC Saturation}

LLM activations exhibit systematic \emph{outlier channels}~\cite{dettmers2022llmint8}: certain feature dimensions consistently carry magnitudes far exceeding the median.
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:sat_heterogeneity}, saturation rates vary dramatically across layer types---$W_{qkv}$/$W_{fc1}$ saturate near 100\% while $W_{out}$/$W_{fc2}$ remain below 20\%---and this pattern persists from OPT-125M to OPT-1.3B.
A widely used proxy for layer difficulty is the \emph{max-clip saturation rate}:
\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig2_saturation_heterogeneity}
  \caption{Per-layer max-clip ADC saturation rate at 7-bit. (a)~OPT-125M (73 layers): $W_{qkv}$/$W_{fc1}$ saturate near 100\%. (b)~OPT-1.3B (145 layers): the same pattern persists. In both models, $W_{out}$/$W_{fc2}$ remain below 20\%.}
  \label{fig:sat_heterogeneity}
\end{figure}
\begin{equation}
  s_i^\text{max} = \Pr\bigl[|y_{ij}| > V_\text{FS}^\text{max}\bigr]
  \label{eq:sat_rate}
\end{equation}
This proxy (Eq.~\ref{eq:sat_rate}) suggests that layers with high $s_i^\text{max}$ require more ADC bits.
SmoothQuant~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant} mitigates outliers by migrating activation difficulty to weights.
However, as we demonstrate in Sec.~\ref{sec:results}, $s_i^\text{max}$ does not reliably predict the accuracy impact of reducing ADC resolution.

\subsection{Related Work}

\textit{CIM for LLMs.}
PRIME~\cite{chi2016prime} and ISAAC~\cite{shafiee2016isaac} pioneered CIM for CNNs; recent surveys~\cite{wolters2024memory} identify ADC as the bottleneck when scaling CIM to LLMs.
FlexCiM~\cite{flexcim2025} and P3-LLM~\cite{p3llm2025} address LLM CIM inference but do not optimize per-layer ADC precision.

\textit{ADC-Aware CIM.}
Azamat et al.~\cite{azamat2023partial} and CIM$^2$PQ~\cite{sun2024cim2pq} apply mixed-precision ADC to CNN accelerators.
Chen et al.~\cite{chen2025sensitivity} employ Hessian-based sensitivity for ReRAM CIM; in our LLM ADC setting, Hessian trace is uncorrelated ($\rho{=}0.20$) and saturation is anti-correlated ($\rho{=}{-}0.70$) with measured sensitivity.
Khwa et al.~\cite{khwa2025nature_mixed_cim} and MXFormer~\cite{mxformer2025} validate mixed-precision CIM at processor level.

\textit{LLM Quantization.}
SmoothQuant~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}, GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq}, and AWQ~\cite{lin2023awq} target digital accelerators.
HAWQ~\cite{dong2019hawq} and HAQ~\cite{wang2019haq} use Hessian traces or RL for digital quantization; these proxies do not transfer to CIM ADC noise.
Yan et al.~\cite{yan2023nvcim} address worst-case NVCiM accuracy through training-time noise injection; our work complements theirs with deployment-time allocation.
We employ DNN+NeuroSIM~\cite{chen2018neurosim,peng2019dnn,lee2024neurosim} for circuit-level validation.

\section{Sensitivity Profiling Methodology}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

\subsection{Measurement Protocol}

We define the \emph{measured sensitivity} of layer group $i$ as the perplexity increase caused by reducing its ADC resolution:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_i = \frac{\text{PPL}(\mathbf{b}^{(i)}) - \text{PPL}(\mathbf{b}^\text{ref})}{|g_i|}
  \label{eq:sensitivity_measured}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{b}^\text{ref} = [b_\text{nom}, \ldots, b_\text{nom}]$ is the uniform baseline and $\mathbf{b}^{(i)}$ reduces only group $i$ to $b_\text{prb}$ while keeping all other layers at $b_\text{nom}$.
Fig.~\ref{fig:noise_path} illustrates our qualitative hypothesis that different linear groups induce distinct noise-propagation paths through a decoder block.
For efficiency, we adopt group-level measurement (Eq.~\ref{eq:sensitivity_measured})---reducing all layers of the same functional type simultaneously---yielding only $G = 5$ measurements rather than 73.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:sensitivity} formalizes the protocol.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_noise_path}
  \caption{Qualitative noise-propagation hypothesis for the saturation--sensitivity inversion. Noise injected at $W_{qkv}$ passes through attention-score computation, softmax/head mixing, $W_{out}$, and residual/normalization paths before affecting later states, which is consistent with lower measured sensitivity in the OPT-125M 7b$\rightarrow$6b profiling example. In contrast, noise injected at $W_{fc2}$ enters the residual stream after the FFN and can affect subsequent decoder blocks, which is consistent with higher measured sensitivity. The diagram illustrates an interpretation of the measured trend rather than a formal proof.}
  \label{fig:noise_path}
\end{figure}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Group-Level ADC Sensitivity Measurement}
\label{alg:sensitivity}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Model $\mathcal{M}$, calibration data $\mathcal{D}_\text{cal}$, profiling data $\mathcal{D}_\text{prof}$, nominal bits $b_\text{nom}$, probe bits $b_\text{prb}$
\ENSURE Per-group measured sensitivity $\{\sigma_g\}_{g=1}^G$
\STATE Partition $N$ layers into $G$ groups by functional type
\STATE Calibrate ADC full-scale $V_{\text{FS},i}$ for each layer $i$ using $\mathcal{D}_\text{cal}$
\STATE $\mathbf{b}^\text{ref} \leftarrow [b_\text{nom}] \times N$
\STATE $\text{PPL}_\text{ref} \leftarrow \text{Eval}(\mathcal{M}, \mathbf{b}^\text{ref}, \mathcal{D}_\text{prof})$
\FOR{each group $g = 1, \ldots, G$}
  \STATE $\mathbf{b}^{(g)} \leftarrow \mathbf{b}^\text{ref}$; set $b^{(g)}_i = b_\text{prb}$ for all $i \in g$
  \STATE $\text{PPL}_g \leftarrow \text{Eval}(\mathcal{M}, \mathbf{b}^{(g)}, \mathcal{D}_\text{prof})$
  \STATE $\sigma_g \leftarrow (\text{PPL}_g - \text{PPL}_\text{ref}) / |g|$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\{\sigma_g\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Group-Level Granularity Validation}

We validate group-level profiling with depth-bin analysis and per-layer spot-checks on three representative blocks (Fig.~\ref{fig:grouping}).
FFN layers are consistently more sensitive than attention layers at every depth position; sensitivity generally decreases with depth, though $W_{fc2}$ remains above 0.4 $\Delta$PPL even at Late positions.
We further compare group-ILP against per-layer ILP (73 individual sensitivity measurements): group-ILP outperforms per-layer ILP at moderate budgets ($\leq$30\% savings) because group-level averaging reduces measurement noise from limited evaluation batches.
Per-layer ILP recovers its advantage only at aggressive budgets ($>$40\%).

  \label{fig:grouping}

We partition the WikiText-2~\cite{merity2017pointer} evaluation set into three non-overlapping splits: $\mathcal{D}_\text{cal}$ (4 batches) for ADC full-scale calibration, $\mathcal{D}_\text{prof}$ (10 batches, ${\approx}$5K tokens) for sensitivity profiling (Algorithm~\ref{alg:sensitivity}), and $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$ (100 batches, ${\approx}$51K tokens) for all final PPL evaluations reported in Table~\ref{tab:mixed_results}.
All sequences are 512 tokens with p99-clip ADC calibration.
The profiling and test sets do not overlap; allocation decisions are made solely on $\mathcal{D}_\text{prof}$.

\section{Area-Aware Bit Budgeting}
\label{sec:allocation}

We formulate the budgeted bit assignment as an integer linear program (ILP) that integrates measured sensitivities, layer-specific area coefficients, and bit-choice constraints into a single optimization step.

\subsection{ILP Formulation}

Each layer inherits the measured sensitivity of its functional group: $\sigma_i = \sigma_g$ for all layers $i \in g$.
Given $N$ layers with these sensitivities and a target ADC area budget $B$, we minimize total sensitivity-weighted degradation:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{b}} \sum_i \max(0,\, b_\text{nom} - b_i) \cdot \sigma_i^{+}
  \quad \text{s.t.}\; \sum_i a_i \, 2^{b_i} \leq B,\; b_i \in \mathcal{B}
  \label{eq:ilp}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i^{+} = \max(\sigma_i, 0)$ clamps negative sensitivities to zero (negative values can arise from evaluation noise and would otherwise cause the solver to exploit finite-sample variance),
$b_\text{nom}$ is the nominal bit width (7 for profiling, 10 for deployment), $\mathcal{B}$ is the set of allowed bit widths ($\{4,5,6,7\}$ for the 7b stress-test regime; $\{7,8,9,10\}$ for 10b deployment; in practice the ILP selects only 6 and 7 at the tested moderate budgets), $a_i$ is the layer-specific ADC area coefficient proportional to the number of ADC columns in the NeuroSIM mapping, and the area constraint follows the MLSA model (Eq.~\ref{eq:adc_area}).
The resulting mixed-precision ADC-area saving is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta A_\text{ADC} = \sum_i a_i \bigl(2^{b_\text{nom}} - 2^{b_i^\ast}\bigr)
  \label{eq:area_saving}
\end{equation}
We linearize~(\ref{eq:ilp}) via binary indicator variables $x_{i,b} \in \{0,1\}$ and solve with \texttt{scipy.optimize.milp}.
For OPT-125M (292 binary variables), the ILP solves to proven optimality in under 0.1\,s.
Exhaustive enumeration of all $4^5{=}1{,}024$ group-level configurations confirms that the ILP solution matches the brute-force optimum.

\subsection{Surrogate Validation}

The linear surrogate is approximate for pairwise $\Delta$PPL prediction (Table~\ref{tab:surrogate}, Fig.~\ref{fig:interaction}), but its primary role is \emph{allocation ranking}.
Two strong validation signals confirm its correctness: (1)~exhaustive enumeration verifies that the ILP matches the brute-force optimum (regret\,=\,0.0); (2)~end-to-end measured PPL independently validates allocation quality.
The surrogate suffices because moderate-budget ILP reduces only one or two groups, where individual measurements are exact by construction.

\subsection{Computational Cost}

Sensitivity measurement requires $G{+}1 = 6$ evaluation runs; each run evaluates the full $\mathcal{D}_\text{prof}$ split of 10 batches (${\approx}$5K tokens).
The ILP involves $N \times |\mathcal{B}|$ binary variables (292 for OPT-125M, $<$0.1\,s; 580 for OPT-1.3B, $<$0.2\,s).
The total profiling overhead is negligible compared to deployment.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:results}

We validate the complete allocation pipeline with NeuroSIM~\cite{chen2018neurosim} circuit-level analysis (45\,nm CMOS, $128{\times}128$ RRAM crossbar, MLSA-type ADC, 1-bit DAC input, INT8 weight mapping with bit-slicing), covering area, energy, latency, and downstream task accuracy.

\subsection{Setup and Reference Definitions}

We define four reference operating points.
\textbf{FP32}: no CIM noise or weight quantization.
\textbf{CIM-$b$b}: INT8 weights + $b$-bit ADC with p99-clip calibration.
\textbf{Profiling regime}: CIM-7b$\to$6b, where sensitivity contrasts are largest.
\textbf{Deployment regime}: CIM-8b/9b/10b, where practical chips operate.
The FP32-to-CIM gap is dominated by INT8 weight quantization without fine-tuning, not ADC noise.
To confirm this, we measure an INT8-only control (12-bit ADC, effectively removing ADC noise) alongside CIM-7b and CIM-10b on the same 10-batch split used for the cross-regime sanity check in Fig.~\ref{fig:deployment}; absolute PPL values differ from the 100-batch $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$ of Table~\ref{tab:mixed_results} due to the different evaluation split, but the \emph{relative} INT8-vs-ADC comparison is valid within this matched protocol:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Configuration & PPL \\
\midrule
FP32 (no quantization) & 36.2 \\
INT8-only (12b ADC) & 316.9 \\
INT8 + 10b ADC & 316.9 \\
INT8 + 7b ADC & 317.1 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The INT8-only and CIM-7b baselines differ by only 0.2\,PPL, indicating that ADC clipping contributes negligibly compared with the INT8 weight-quantization gap.
Our goal is not to recover the INT8 quantization loss, but to reduce ADC cost under a fixed CIM quantization floor; improved weight quantization (e.g., GPTQ, AWQ) is orthogonal and can be combined with our allocation flow.
CIM-7b through CIM-10b baselines are nearly identical under a consistent evaluation protocol (Fig.~\ref{fig:deployment}a); this 10-batch cross-regime sweep is used only to validate baseline stability, while all final allocation comparisons are reported on the 100-batch $\mathcal{D}_\text{test}$ in Table~\ref{tab:mixed_results}.
The allocation problem we address---reducing ADC area with minimal \emph{additional} degradation beyond the weight quantization floor---remains practically relevant because the relative PPL differences between allocation methods are consistent and reproducible, and the area savings scale to hundreds of mm$^2$ at practical model sizes.

\subsection{Sensitivity Inversion and Cross-Regime Transfer}

The measured sensitivities (Fig.~\ref{fig:sensitivity}) reveal that saturation rate ranks $W_{qkv}$ and $W_{fc1}$ as the most problematic layers (${\approx}$100\% saturation), yet measured sensitivity ranks them \emph{last} (0.13 and 0.20 $\Delta$PPL/layer at 7b$\to$6b).
$W_{fc2}$ ranks first at 1.41 $\Delta$PPL/layer despite only 19\% saturation---an $11\times$ inversion.
The exact rank correlation varies across adjacent regimes; for non-adjacent and high-bit regime pairs, Spearman $\rho$ ranges from $0.60$ to $0.90$.
More importantly, the allocation-critical ordering is stable: $W_{qkv}$ remains in the low-sensitivity set and FFN layers remain more sensitive than attention layers at every tested operating point.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_sensitivity_heatmap}
  \caption{(a)~Measured sensitivity ($\Delta$PPL/layer) across four operating points (OPT-125M). $W_{fc2}$ is the most sensitive at 3/4 regimes; $W_{qkv}$ is consistently the least. (b)~Saturation proxy vs.\ measured rank: the proxy would protect the \emph{wrong} layers ($\rho = -0.70$).}
  \label{fig:sensitivity}
\end{figure}

We attribute this inversion to qualitative noise-propagation effects rather than treating them as formal proofs (Fig.~\ref{fig:noise_path}).
For $W_{qkv}$, ADC perturbations pass through attention-score computation, softmax/head mixing, $W_{out}$, and residual/normalization paths before affecting later hidden states, which can attenuate their measured impact.
For $W_{fc2}$, perturbations are injected into the residual stream after the FFN and can influence subsequent decoder blocks, consistent with the higher measured sensitivity and the mid-depth sensitivity peak in Fig.~\ref{fig:grouping}a.

The FFN\,$>$\,attention hierarchy holds at all four tested operating points (Fig.~\ref{fig:sensitivity}a).
Fig.~\ref{fig:transfer} visualizes the rank stability and assignment overlap: 7b$\to$6b profiling produces 5/5 group-level agreement with native profiling at all three tested higher-bit regimes, with zero group-level allocation regret (PPL within 0.2\% of baseline).

\subsection{Allocation Results}

Table~\ref{tab:mixed_results} reports measured PPL for all configurations.
To isolate the value of the measured sensitivity \emph{signal} from the ILP \emph{solver}, we run four allocation signals through the same ILP at identical budgets.
At 20.5\% ADC-area savings, profiling-guided ILP ($+$0.7\%) outperforms Hessian-ILP ($+$1.1\%), saturation-ILP ($+$0.9\%), and random allocation ($+$2.6\%$\pm$1.4\%, 20 trials).
For the proxy baselines, we estimate per-group Hessian trace via diagonal Fisher approximation on $\mathcal{D}_\text{prof}$ using the same 10 profiling batches, normalize across groups, and feed the resulting proxy scores to the identical ILP solver; saturation-ILP uses per-group saturation rate as the proxy signal.
The same pattern persists at 30\% savings.
Exhaustive group-level brute-force search confirms that the ILP solution matches the globally optimal group-level configuration.
Fig.~\ref{fig:bit_assignment} visualizes the per-layer assignments; Fig.~\ref{fig:pareto} shows the PPL--area Pareto frontier.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that directly measured layer sensitivity provides a more reliable ADC allocation signal than saturation or Hessian proxies in the CIM regimes tested.
The key empirical finding---a saturation--sensitivity inversion where the highest-saturation layers are the \emph{least} accuracy-sensitive---can be extracted in $G{+}1$ evaluation runs and remains stable across calibration seeds, operating points, and transfer regimes.
On OPT-125M, profiling-guided ILP outperforms saturation-, Hessian-, and random-guided baselines under the same solver, achieving 20\% ADC-area savings at $+$0.7\% relative PPL (Table~\ref{tab:mixed_results}).
On OPT-1.3B, it outperforms saturation-guided allocation at both tested budgets.
Transfer sanity checks confirm that the sensitivity ordering generalizes to higher-bit deployment regimes with negligible accuracy cost.
The practical payoff is ADC-area reduction that scales from tens of mm$^2$ at OPT-125M to hundreds at OPT-1.3B.

\textit{Limitations.}
The linear surrogate preserves correct allocation ranking at moderate (10--30\%) savings but may degrade at aggressive budgets where multi-group interactions become significant.
Group-level profiling is a first-order signal; finer depth-aware refinement may further improve allocation quality.
Better weight quantization (e.g., GPTQ, AWQ) would reduce the absolute FP32-to-CIM gap but would not invalidate the ADC allocation comparison, which operates on relative sensitivity differences above the quantization floor.
All experiments use NeuroSIM simulation; silicon validation and extension to encoder-decoder or MoE architectures remain future work.
